## 1. Core venue thesis

The venue decision is not a logistics detail.

It is the first major trust decision of TCSW 2026.

The physical system determines whether TCSW feels coherent, fundable, navigable, human, and sponsor-worthy.

If the venue model is fragmented, every downstream touchpoint becomes harder:

- sponsor promises become unstable;
- programming capacity becomes unclear;
- attendee flow becomes confusing;
- app-enabled meetups become harder to coordinate;
- signage and maps become more expensive;
- volunteer staffing becomes more complex;
- sponsors lose confidence;
- Angela remains trapped in logistics;
- BETA risks another trust break.

Therefore, the first operational priority is:

> **Make the physical system legible.**

## 2. What the Jun 3 meeting revealed

The Jun 3 Angela / Rachel / Liban conversation made the venue problem explicit.

### 2.1 Location architecture is first-order

Angela named locations as the immediate blocker: how many rooms, how many speakers, what spaces are available, what can be accommodated, and what can be promised back to partners and sponsors.

Until the location architecture is clear, SMS and BETA cannot responsibly finalize:

- programming;
- sponsor packages;
- host commitments;
- attendee communication;
- app connection use cases;
- day-by-day run of show;
- maps;
- wayfinding;
- volunteer staffing.

### 2.2 Prior spread-out models hurt experience

Angela shared feedback that previous TCSW experiences suffered when events were spread across downtown or across too many disconnected locations.

People missed sessions. People missed the people they wanted to meet. Moving between locations created friction.

This is central to trust restoration.

TCSW 2026 should not repeat a model where the schedule looks rich on paper but feels hard to actually attend.

### 2.3 Universities matter, but may not be sufficient as the backbone

Angela initially explored universities because entrepreneurship schools and students need visibility in the startup ecosystem.

The university thesis is valid:

- students and emerging builders matter;
- AI makes student and technical talent especially relevant this year;
- entrepreneurship schools need ecosystem visibility;
- universities can provide credibility, audience, and possible low-cost space.

But the conversation also revealed constraints:

- universities are in session;
- room availability may be limited;
- campus access can be complicated;
- not all entrepreneurship schools can provide usable space;
- U of M may be harder than expected;
- moving attendees between campuses could recreate fragmentation.

Therefore, universities should be treated as one possible venue source and programming partner, not automatically the full venue backbone.

### 2.4 A showcase model may be stronger than a campus-host model

Rachel and Liban surfaced an important alternative:

Instead of requiring the audience to move to universities, TCSW can invite universities and students into a central venue or anchor location as featured participants.

This preserves university visibility while reducing attendee friction.

Working principle:

> **Bring the universities into the room before forcing the room to travel to the universities.**

### 2.5 In-kind venue support is critical

Venue cost is one of the biggest budget variables.

Free or reduced-cost space can materially change the funding model.

However, in-kind space is only valuable if it is operationally usable:

- right size;
- right location;
- right rooms;
- right access;
- right AV;
- right dates;
- right sponsor fit;
- right attendee flow.

## 3. Venue strategy principles

### 3.1 Legibility over novelty

A cool venue is not enough.

The attendee must understand where to go, when to arrive, where to meet people, and how to move through the day.

### 3.2 Concentration over sprawl

TCSW should prioritize concentrated location architecture.

The fewer disconnected moves required, the stronger the attendee experience.

### 3.3 Anchor first, satellites second

Start with an anchor venue or anchor hub.

Only add satellite venues if they increase value without breaking flow.

### 3.4 Venue model must match funding reality

Do not design a venue model that requires cash the event does not have.

Venue commitments should be linked to funding thresholds in `budget-model.md`.

### 3.5 Venue model must support sponsor value

Sponsors need confidence that their role can be delivered.

Venue strategy should help answer:

- where will sponsors be visible?
- where will sponsor-hosted moments happen?
- where will attendees gather naturally?
- where can small-group connection happen?
- what rooms can actually support promised programming?

### 3.6 Venue model must support SMS app use cases

The SMS app connection layer works best when people can find small groups nearby.

The venue model should create natural meetup zones:

- lobby tables;
- breakfast areas;
- post-session gathering spots;
- sponsor-supported connection zones;
- outdoor / courtyard options if available;
- nearby cafes / partner venues;
- clearly named rooms and landmarks.

### 3.7 Reduce transportation complexity

Shuttles introduce cost, liability, operational burden, and timing risk.

Use shuttles only if they are clearly funded, staffed, insured, and necessary.

Default position:

> **Design the event so attendees do not need managed transportation to have a good experience.**

## 4. Venue model options

## 4.1 Model A — Single anchor venue

### Description

Most TCSW programming happens in one primary location.

### Best for

- trust restoration;
- simple maps;
- sponsor confidence;
- attendee ease;
- app-enabled meetup discovery;
- first-time attendee experience;
- reduced staffing complexity.

### Pros

- easiest to understand;
- easiest to market;
- strongest attendee flow;
- most sponsor-friendly;
- easier check-in and badge distribution;
- easier signage and wayfinding;
- easier volunteer coordination.

### Cons

- requires a venue with enough rooms and capacity;
- may be expensive;
- may limit university / campus symbolism;
- may reduce neighborhood diversity.

### Use if

A single affordable or in-kind venue can provide enough rooms, gathering areas, and sponsor visibility.

---

## 4.2 Model B — Anchor hub + nearby satellites

### Description

One primary venue serves as home base, supported by nearby walking-distance partner venues.

### Best for

- balancing concentration with program diversity;
- partner-hosted sessions;
- sponsor activations;
- evening events;
- university / community participation without full campus dependency.

### Pros

- keeps the week legible;
- allows more rooms;
- enables community partners to host;
- reduces pressure on one venue;
- creates neighborhood energy;
- supports small-group meetups before and after sessions.

### Cons

- requires strong maps and signage;
- requires clear time buffers;
- requires venue captains;
- requires careful schedule design;
- can become fragmented if satellites are too far apart.

### Use if

Anchor venue is strong but not large enough for the full program, and nearby satellites are genuinely walkable.

---

## 4.3 Model C — Day-by-day campus model

### Description

Each day is hosted at or near a different university or campus.

### Best for

- university visibility;
- student engagement;
- education / talent framing;
- lower venue cost if campuses provide space in-kind.

### Pros

- showcases multiple institutions;
- may unlock in-kind rooms;
- gives students easier access;
- aligns with entrepreneurship school visibility;
- creates daily thematic identity.

### Cons

- campuses are in session;
- attendee access may be complicated;
- room availability may be limited;
- audience may fragment by day;
- sponsor visibility may reset daily;
- maps and wayfinding become harder;
- can recreate the spread-out problem if not tightly managed.

### Use if

Each campus can provide a clear, usable, concentrated footprint and the day can be designed as a self-contained experience.

---

## 4.4 Model D — Partner-distributed model

### Description

Programming is distributed across many partner spaces, coworking spaces, breweries, offices, and community venues.

### Best for

- low cash budget;
- organic community participation;
- partner-owned rooms;
- evening events;
- hyperlocal feel.

### Pros

- low venue cost potential;
- high community ownership;
- many partners can host;
- can feel organic and alive.

### Cons

- highest fragmentation risk;
- hardest for attendees to navigate;
- hardest for sponsors to understand;
- hardest for SMS to produce coherently;
- requires more venue captains;
- risks repeating the old trust issue.

### Use if

Funding is low and expectations are intentionally set around a smaller, community-hosted model.

---

## 4.5 Model E — Hybrid compact district

### Description

A concentrated district becomes the venue: one anchor location plus a tight set of nearby campuses, coworking spaces, breweries, and partner rooms.

### Best for

- balancing budget, community ownership, and coherence;
- creating a neighborhood feel;
- avoiding one expensive venue;
- supporting small-group discovery.

### Pros

- more flexible than single anchor;
- more legible than full distribution;
- sponsor-friendly if well mapped;
- can support The Great Reunion as a physical campus;
- can make the SMS app useful for nearby meetups.

### Cons

- requires disciplined location radius;
- requires strong maps and volunteer support;
- requires time-buffered programming;
- can fail if the district is too spread out.

### Use if

SMS can define a tight radius and keep the event walkable, understandable, and well-supported.

---

## 5. Recommended direction

The recommended working direction is:

> **Anchor hub + nearby satellites, with a bias toward a compact district.**

This gives TCSW 2026 the best balance of:

- trust restoration;
- attendee legibility;
- sponsor confidence;
- venue cost flexibility;
- community participation;
- app-enabled small-group connection;
- university visibility without total campus dependency.

Avoid two extremes:

1. **Over-centralized expensive venue** that breaks the budget.
2. **Over-distributed community sprawl** that breaks the attendee experience.

The goal is a compact, understandable physical system that feels like a gathering point.

## 6. Venue evaluation criteria

Every venue should be evaluated against the same criteria.

| Criteria | Question | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Capacity | Can it hold the expected audience and room needs? | High |
| Room mix | Does it have main room + breakouts + gathering areas? | High |
| Cost | Is it affordable or in-kind? | High |
| Availability | Is it available Sept. 14–18 or relevant days? | High |
| Access | Can attendees enter easily? | High |
| Proximity | Is it close to other useful venues / transit / parking? | High |
| Sponsor fit | Can sponsors be visible without harming experience? | Medium / High |
| App fit | Are there clear meetup zones and nearby small-group spaces? | Medium / High |
| AV / tech | Can it support sessions without expensive add-ons? | Medium |
| Food / hospitality | Can basic hospitality be provided? | Medium |
| Brand fit | Does it feel credible for Minnesota innovation? | Medium |
| University / talent fit | Does it support student / university engagement? | Medium |
| Risk | What could go wrong? | High |

## 9. Day-by-day venue implications

### Monday — MN Cup Grand Finale

Working assumption:

- Monday is anchored by MN Cup Grand Finale.
- TCSW registration / badge access may be available there if feasible.
- BETA should avoid overprogramming Monday beyond MN Cup.

Venue implications:

- badge / check-in table;
- signage;
- clear transition into Tuesday;
- attendee communication about where the week truly begins.

### Tuesday — Industry Day

Venue needs:

- room mix for industry clusters;
- sponsor visibility;
- enough space for industry-specific partner programming;
- clear attendee navigation;
- proximity to happy hour / meetup spaces.

Risk:

Industry Day could become fragmented if each industry is in a different disconnected location.

### Wednesday — Innovation / AI Day

Venue needs:

- main room for AI / innovation anchor;
- demo or workshop spaces;
- student / engineer / founder connection areas;
- strong AV;
- app-enabled small-group meetup zones.

Risk:

AI programming can overpromise or become hype-heavy. Venue should support useful, applied, human-scale conversations.

### Thursday — Investor Day

Venue needs:

- investor / founder networking spaces;
- private or semi-private conversation zones;
- clear sponsor presence;
- room for capital education;
- controlled flow to protect founder trust.

Risk:

Do not create pay-to-access or chaotic investor/founder dynamics.

### Friday — Give Back / Hack / Community Day

Venue needs:

- collaborative work space;
- tables;
- power;
- Wi-Fi;
- food / hydration;
- mentor / team areas;
- final gathering or closing moment.

Risk:

Hack / give-back programming requires a different physical setup than panels.

## 10. Venue / sponsor relationship

Venue strategy directly affects sponsor strategy.

Sponsors need to know:

- where the event is happening;
- what kind of audience flow exists;
- where they can be recognized;
- whether they can host a session or meetup;
- whether their sponsored role has physical meaning;
- whether the event feels credible enough to support.

Before sponsor promises are made, venue status must be checked.

Sponsor promise rule:

> **No room, no promise.**

## 14. Outreach questions for venues

Use these questions in venue calls.

### Basic availability

1. Are you available during Sept. 14–18, 2026?
2. Which dates and times are available?
3. Are there conflicts with classes, events, campus operations, or venue holds?

### Space inventory

4. What rooms are available?
5. What are the capacities by room?
6. What layouts are possible?
7. Is there a main gathering area?
8. Are there breakout rooms?
9. Are there informal meetup areas?
10. Is there outdoor or overflow space?

### Cost / in-kind

11. What is the rental cost?
12. Are deposits required?
13. Can any space be donated or discounted in-kind?
14. What services are included?
15. What services are additional costs?

### Access

16. How do attendees enter?
17. Is check-in possible near the entrance?
18. Is the venue ADA accessible?
19. What are parking and transit options?
20. Are there security or ID requirements?

### AV / tech

21. What AV is included?
22. Are projectors, microphones, screens, and Wi-Fi included?
23. Is tech staff required?
24. Are outside AV vendors allowed?

### Food / hospitality

25. Can outside food be brought in?
26. Is catering required through the venue?
27. Are coffee / water / breakfast options available?
28. Are there restrictions on alcohol or evening events?

### Sponsor / signage

29. Can sponsor signage be placed on site?
30. Are there restrictions on logos, banners, tables, or activations?
31. Can sponsors host small gatherings in the space?

### Risk / logistics

32. Is insurance required?
33. Are permits required?
34. What staffing does the venue require?
35. What is the cancellation policy?
36. What deadlines apply?

## 16. Venue risk register

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Venue model too spread out | Attendees miss sessions and people; trust breaks | Prioritize anchor hub / compact district |
| University rooms unavailable | Programming architecture collapses | Treat universities as partners, not sole backbone |
| Venue costs too high | Budget consumed before production is staffed | Prioritize in-kind and backup options |
| Sponsor promises made too early | Sponsor trust risk | No room, no promise |
| Check-in flow unclear | First impression suffers | Design check-in before public launch |
| Wayfinding weak | Attendees feel lost | Map, signage, app labels, venue captains |
| AV costs hidden | Budget surprise | Ask AV questions early |
| Food rules restrictive | Hospitality costs rise | Validate catering rules early |
| App meetups unsupported by layout | Connection layer underperforms | Identify meetup zones during venue review |
| Shuttle dependency emerges | Liability and logistics risk | Avoid managed transportation unless funded and necessary |
| Accessibility overlooked | Attendee exclusion and reputational risk | ADA and access review required |

## 17. Venue strategy by funding level

### Minimum viable venue model

- one low-cost / in-kind anchor venue if possible;
- few or no satellites;
- limited official programming;
- simple check-in;
- basic signage;
- low-complexity app meetup zones;
- transparent expectations.

### Target venue model

- strong anchor hub;
- nearby walkable satellites only if useful;
- clear day-by-day architecture;
- sponsor visibility areas;
- first-time attendee gathering points;
- maps and signage;
- venue captains;
- app-friendly connection zones.

### Stretch venue model

- higher-quality anchor venue or district;
- stronger environmental design;
- sponsor lounges / meeting areas;
- higher-quality signage and maps;
- stronger hospitality;
- media capture zones;
- expanded app-enabled gathering support.
