## 1. Core model

SMS proposes to serve as the:

> **End-to-End Production & Community Experience Partner for Twin Cities Startup Week 2026.**

This model is designed to protect BETA while giving SMS enough authority to carry the production responsibility responsibly.

The partnership is not a takeover of TCSW.

The partnership is an operating structure that allows:

- BETA to remain the institutional steward;
- Angela to focus on sponsor closing, board alignment, and executive relationships;
- SMS to carry the integrated production and community experience layer;
- sponsors to see a credible, fundable, professionally held event;
- attendees and partners to experience a coherent public journey.

Clean division:

> **BETA holds the institutional trust. Angela closes and stewards relationships. SMS produces the coherent public experience.**

---

## 2. Why this model is necessary

TCSW 2026 is not simply a programming challenge.

It is an integrated trust, funding, operations, marketing, venue, sponsor, and attendee-experience challenge.

The core risk is fragmentation:

- fragmented venues;
- fragmented programming;
- fragmented sponsor promises;
- fragmented marketing;
- fragmented attendee experience;
- fragmented communication;
- fragmented ownership.

Fragmentation is how trust breaks.

Therefore, the partnership must align responsibility with authority.

> **End-to-end responsibility requires end-to-end authority.**

SMS cannot be accountable for the quality of the public experience if SMS does not have meaningful authority over the touchpoints that create that experience.

---

## 3. Partnership principles

### 3.1 BETA remains the steward

BETA is the long-term institutional home of TCSW.

BETA's nonprofit status, history, sponsor relationships, founder ecosystem credibility, and 14-year community equity must be protected.

### 3.2 SMS is the production and community experience engine

SMS is responsible for making the 2026 public experience coherent, fundable, operationally legible, and human.

SMS should be credited and compensated for real production responsibility while avoiding any perception that SMS is capturing BETA's equity.

### 3.3 Angela should be elevated out of logistics

Angela's highest-value contribution is not spreadsheet management or room-by-room logistics.

Angela's highest-value contribution is institutional leadership, sponsor closing, relationship stewardship, board alignment, and trust restoration.

### 3.4 Every touchpoint must increase value

The partnership should improve perceived and actual value across:

- sponsor outreach;
- landing page;
- registration;
- host intake;
- speaker experience;
- venue selection;
- marketing assets;
- app-enabled connection;
- attendee communication;
- volunteer experience;
- sponsor delivery;
- post-event continuity.

### 3.5 No overpromising

TCSW 2026 must rebuild trust.

Every public promise should be tied to operational reality: venue capacity, funding status, staffing, schedule feasibility, and partner commitments.

## 4. Role architecture

## 4.1 BETA owns institutional stewardship

BETA owns:

- TCSW brand stewardship;
- nonprofit legitimacy;
- sponsor contract authority;
- board governance;
- mission alignment;
- founder ecosystem credibility;
- existing BETA/TCSW relationships;
- long-term trust restoration;
- final approval of major institutional commitments.

BETA should not be expected to carry detailed event production internally unless the scope is reduced to match its actual staff capacity.

## 4.2 Angela owns executive relationships and sponsor closing

Angela owns:

- final sponsor closing conversations;
- high-level partner relationships;
- board communication;
- executive narrative;
- BETA's public leadership posture;
- final nonprofit mission alignment;
- escalation decisions where BETA's institutional risk is involved.

SMS supports Angela by making the event more fundable, legible, and credible.

## 4.3 SMS owns production and community experience

SMS owns:

- venue sourcing and procurement support;
- venue comparison and recommendation process;
- location architecture;
- programming architecture;
- host / speaker intake process support;
- run-of-show planning;
- attendee journey design;
- sponsor experience design;
- volunteer experience design;
- brand and marketing asset production;
- landing page and campaign support;
- lead-up engagement design;
- SMS app connection layer;
- trackers, milestones, risks, blockers, and delivery cadence;
- production team coordination;
- first 30-day execution sprint.

SMS is responsible for turning the public experience into a coherent system.

## 5. Scope of work

### 5.1 Venue and location architecture

SMS will support the sourcing, evaluation, and procurement of the right venue model.

Responsibilities include:

- build venue pipeline;
- assess capacity, cost, accessibility, proximity, brand fit, and sponsor fit;
- compare university, stadium, coworking, brewery, civic, and partner venue options;
- recommend anchor venue strategy;
- map rooms and capacities;
- identify daily location architecture;
- determine which promises can be made safely based on space;
- coordinate with venue contacts as assigned.

First priority:

> Make the physical system legible.

### 5.2 Programming architecture

SMS will develop the architecture of the program, not simply collect sessions.

Responsibilities include:

- day-by-day structure;
- session categories;
- anchor programming logic;
- host and speaker intake refinement;
- programming quality standards;
- run-of-show templates;
- transition logic;
- host expectations;
- audience-fit guidance;
- conflict reduction across sessions;
- program feasibility relative to venues.

Programming should support the public thesis:

> **The Great Reunion — the act of coming together again.**

### 5.3 Brand, campaign, and marketing assets

SMS will support the public perception reset of TCSW.

Responsibilities include:

- campaign language;
- landing page structure;
- visual system direction;
- shareable graphics;
- sponsor-facing assets;
- host-facing assets;
- attendee communications;
- maps and wayfinding assets;
- social templates;
- announcement support.

The goal is not decoration.

The goal is to make TCSW feel credible, valuable, and shareable before September.

### 5.4 Sponsor support

Angela remains the sponsor closer.

SMS supports sponsor development by making the sponsor story more compelling and operationally grounded.

SMS responsibilities include:

- sponsor target intelligence;
- sponsor narrative;
- sponsor-role framing;
- sponsor package structure support;
- sponsor experience mapping;
- sponsor deliverable feasibility review;
- sponsor activation ideas tied to actual venue/program capacity;
- support materials for Angela's sponsor conversations.

SMS should not make final financial commitments to sponsors without Angela/BETA approval.

### 5.5 SMS app connection layer

The SMS app is positioned as the community connection layer, not the official ticketing or schedule replacement.

Responsibilities include:

- define TCSW-specific use cases;
- support small-group meetup discovery;
- enable first-time attendee entry points;
- support post-session continuation circles;
- support sponsor-hosted community moments;
- support founder/customer discovery conversations;
- support pre-event and post-event continuity;
- coordinate any needed integration boundaries with existing TCSW platforms.

The product thesis:

> **The SMS app helps large gatherings feel human.**

### 5.6 Operating cadence and delivery discipline

SMS will run the operating rhythm required to move from ambiguity to execution.

Responsibilities include:

- weekly core operating meeting;
- decision log;
- risk register;
- venue tracker;
- sponsor-support tracker;
- programming tracker;
- action-owner tracker;
- weekly status summary;
- escalation path for blockers;
- 30-day sprint planning.

## 6. Non-scope / retained by BETA

Unless otherwise agreed, BETA retains responsibility for:

- final sponsor contracts;
- final nonprofit legal commitments;
- board governance;
- existing TCSW/BETA financial accounts;
- existing platform/vendor commitments;
- final approval of major public claims;
- final approval of sponsor deliverables;
- final approval of TCSW brand usage;
- final approval of any contract that binds BETA financially or legally.

SMS may support these areas, but should not own them unless formally authorized.

## 7. Decision-rights model

The partnership should use a clear decision-rights matrix.

| Area | Recommendation owner | Final decision owner | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Venue shortlist | SMS | Angela / BETA | SMS evaluates and recommends; BETA approves contractual commitments |
| Venue contract | BETA with SMS support | BETA | BETA signs unless otherwise agreed |
| Day architecture | SMS | Angela / BETA | Must align with mission, sponsor commitments, and venue capacity |
| Programming acceptance | SMS with Angela input | Shared | Criteria should be documented before intake expands |
| Sponsor contracts | Angela / BETA | BETA | SMS supports narrative and package structure |
| Sponsor promises | SMS feasibility review | Angela / BETA | Avoid promises not grounded in execution reality |
| Brand direction | SMS | Shared | BETA/TCSW brand equity must be protected |
| Public campaign copy | SMS drafts | Angela / BETA approves | Especially for institutional claims |
| SMS app role | SMS | Shared | Must remain complementary to official platform |
| Budget tradeoffs | SMS recommends | BETA approves | Needs minimum / target / stretch budget model |
| Volunteer model | SMS recommends | Shared | Must match production reality |
| Board updates | Angela | Angela / BETA | SMS can provide status inputs |

Principle:

> SMS should own recommendations and execution for the experience layer. BETA should retain final authority where institutional, legal, financial, or brand risk is involved.

## 8. Compensation model to define

This model cannot work as unpaid enthusiasm.

SMS must be compensated because SMS is being asked to carry real production responsibility.

The proposal should define:

- immediate retainer or activation fee;
- monthly production fee through September;
- contractor / staffing budget;
- creative production budget;
- product / engineering budget, if applicable;
- reimbursable expenses;
- sponsorship-contingent upside, if any;
- deferred compensation, if any;
- what happens if funding arrives late;
- what happens if funding arrives below target.

Guardrail:

> SMS should not absorb BETA's organizational risk through unpaid labor without clear compensation, authority, and risk boundaries.

## 9. Risk allocation

The proposal must protect both parties.

| Risk | Primary concern | Proposed handling |
|---|---|---|
| Funding risk | Sponsor dollars may arrive late or below target | Define minimum viable budget, target budget, stretch budget, and go/no-go thresholds |
| Execution risk | Event complexity exceeds available capacity | SMS owns production layer with paid team and contractors |
| Brand risk | SMS may appear to capture BETA equity | BETA remains institutional steward; SMS is production/community experience partner |
| Nonprofit/for-profit risk | Exchange may feel imbalanced | Agreement should value BETA equity and SMS labor explicitly |
| Sponsor risk | Sponsors may be promised too much | Sponsor promises must pass feasibility review |
| Venue risk | Venue model may fragment attendee experience | Venue strategy must prioritize legibility, proximity, and capacity |
| App risk | App may be perceived as competing platform | SMS app is connection layer, not official ticketing/schedule replacement |
| Timeline risk | September 14 is close | First 30-day sprint must focus on venue, funding, operating model, and campaign spine |
| Governance risk | Board may lack clarity | Formal proposal should include decision rights and reporting cadence |

The immediate approval path should be lightweight but explicit.

Angela and Linda align that SMS should produce a formal end-to-end partnership proposal.

SMS delivers:

- scope of work;
- budget model;
- compensation model;
- decision-rights matrix;
- risk allocation;
- first 30-day sprint;
- sponsor-support narrative;
- venue strategy.

### Step 3 — BETA review

Angela and Linda determine whether full board review is required or whether board chair / executive authority can authorize next steps.

### Step 4 — Operating agreement

BETA and SMS finalize a written agreement that aligns:

- scope;
- authority;
- compensation;
- credit;
- risk;
- approval checkpoints;
- termination / fallback terms.

### Step 5 — Sprint begins

SMS begins carrying the production layer immediately after written authorization.

## 13. Board-safe language

Use:

> **SMS is not replacing BETA. SMS is helping BETA deliver TCSW in a way that protects BETA's nonprofit equity, restores trust, and gives Angela the capacity to focus on sponsor relationships and institutional stewardship.**

Use:

> **BETA remains the steward. SMS becomes the production and community experience engine.**

Use:

> **This model is designed to align responsibility, authority, funding, and risk so TCSW 2026 can be believable, fundable, and deliverable.**

Avoid:

- SMS takes over TCSW;
- SMS owns TCSW;
- SMS controls BETA's event;
- TCSW is our app launchpad;
- BETA needs rescue;
- we can do everything without funding.

## 15. Final operating thesis

The partnership should be built around this thesis:

> **TCSW 2026 is not simply a week of events. It is the rebuild of Minnesota's startup and technology gathering point. For that rebuild to be fundable and trustworthy, every public touchpoint must increase perceived and actual value. BETA should remain the institutional steward. SMS should carry the end-to-end production and community experience layer with clear authority, compensation, decision rights, and risk boundaries.**

This is the model required to make TCSW 2026 believable, fundable, and deliverable without putting BETA back in harm's way.
