## 1. Core funding thesis

The funding problem is not only a scarcity problem.

It is a **valuation and belief problem**.

TCSW has latent value because it is one of the few existing containers that can credibly gather Minnesota's founders, funders, technologists, universities, students, corporate innovators, civic partners, and ecosystem builders around the future of the state.

The challenge is that this value has not been made visible enough, coherent enough, or trustworthy enough for sponsors to fund it at its true potential.

Therefore, the funding strategy must do more than ask for sponsorship.

It must revalue TCSW in the public imagination.

> **We are not raising money for an event. We are inviting funders to visibly help rebuild Minnesota's startup and technology gathering point.**

## 2. What Angela revealed about the funding context

The Jun 3 conversation clarified several realities.

### 2.1 There are limited dedicated funds

BETA does not currently have a large dedicated operating budget for TCSW 2026.

Angela is working from limited BETA funds while sponsorship dollars are expected to come in over time.

This creates a timing problem: the event must become fundable while it is being built.

### 2.2 Last year was financially unhealthy

The previous year taxed BETA financially and operationally.

TCSW cannot be funded by simply asking BETA to absorb more risk.

The 2026 model must protect BETA from repeating a financially unsustainable pattern.

### 2.3 Sponsors need trust restored

Some sponsor relationships require repair because past commitments did not always feel fulfilled.

Angela is carrying a trust-restoration burden this year. She is prepared to be generous with certain sponsors in order to rebuild credibility.

That generosity must be disciplined. It should not create new overpromises.

### 2.4 Funding and operations are linked

Angela cannot fundraise effectively while also holding logistics, venues, schedule architecture, sponsor experience, marketing, and event production details.

SMS's production role is therefore part of the funding strategy.

SMS helps create the thing Angela can confidently sell.

### 2.5 In-kind value matters

Free or reduced-cost venue space, technology, marketing support, partner programming, and community activation can reduce cash burden.

But in-kind contributions must be organized, tracked, and valued. Otherwise they remain invisible and difficult to manage.

## 3. The funding problem reframed

Weak framing:

> "TCSW needs money to happen."

Strong framing:

> **"TCSW 2026 is the rebuild of Minnesota's startup and technology gathering point. This is the year for sponsors, institutions, and ecosystem partners to be visibly associated with bringing the community together again."**

The first frame creates charity energy.

The second frame creates civic, strategic, and reputational value.

Sponsors do not primarily fund need. They fund:

- momentum;
- credibility;
- visibility;
- relevance;
- access;
- trust;
- status;
- talent connection;
- founder connection;
- industry positioning;
- association with a meaningful comeback.

The funding strategy must sell those values without overpromising.

## 4. What sponsors are actually buying

Sponsors are not buying only logo placement.

They are buying a visible role in **The Great Reunion**: the act of Minnesota's startup and technology ecosystem coming together again.

### 4.1 Civic role

Sponsors help Minnesota's builders come back into relationship.

This matters for companies that want to be seen as serious contributors to the local innovation economy.

### 4.2 Access

Sponsors gain access to founders, operators, students, investors, university talent, emerging AI builders, and ecosystem leaders.

Access must be designed through real programming and connection formats, not promised vaguely.

### 4.3 Visibility

Sponsors attach their names to the comeback year of TCSW.

Visibility should be delivered through campaign assets, landing page placement, on-site presence, sponsor-hosted moments, app-enabled connection opportunities, and post-event recap.

### 4.4 Talent and future-of-work relevance

AI has created a unique reason for companies to engage students, engineers, technical talent, operators, and founders.

TCSW can help sponsors understand and connect with the people shaping the next wave of work and technology in Minnesota.

### 4.5 Trust restoration

Sponsors can help rebuild a shared institution.

This is especially relevant for past sponsors who may have previously supported TCSW and now need a reason to believe the event has been rebuilt with discipline.

### 4.6 Sponsor story

A sponsor should be able to say internally:

> **"We are not just sponsoring another event. We are helping rebuild the gathering point for Minnesota's startup and technology ecosystem at a moment when AI, talent, founders, universities, and corporate innovation are all converging."**

## 5. Funding strategy principles

### 5.1 Value before ask

Do not lead with financial need.

Lead with the moment:

- BETA is coming back;
- TCSW is being rebuilt;
- Minnesota's builders need to gather;
- AI makes this year unusually important;
- SMS is bringing production, creative, product, and community experience capacity;
- sponsors have a limited chance to be seen as founding partners of the rebuild.

### 5.2 Believability before scale

Do not promise a massive event before the venue, budget, and operational model are credible.

Sponsors should see:

- venue strategy;
- production team;
- campaign spine;
- app connection layer;
- sponsor role clarity;
- risk discipline;
- first 30-day execution plan.

### 5.3 Role-based sponsorship over generic tiers

Avoid only Bronze / Silver / Gold language.

Sponsors should be invited into roles:

- Founding Rebuild Partner;
- Great Reunion Partner;
- Industry Day Partner;
- AI + Innovation Partner;
- Founder Access Partner;
- Investor Day Partner;
- Student + Talent Partner;
- Community Connection Partner;
- Venue / Hospitality Partner;
- App-Enabled Connection Partner;
- Give Back / Hack Partner.

These role names are working language and must be refined before public use.

### 5.4 No ungrounded sponsor promises

Every sponsor benefit must be tied to operational capacity.

Do not promise:

- attendance numbers not yet supported;
- rooms not yet secured;
- sponsor activations without staffing;
- audience segments without programming strategy;
- app engagement before product readiness;
- media reach without a media plan.

### 5.5 BETA must be protected

The funding strategy must prevent BETA from carrying disproportionate risk.

BETA's nonprofit equity is real value. Sponsor dollars should protect and strengthen that equity, not extract from it.

### 5.6 SMS must be compensated

SMS cannot carry end-to-end production as unpaid enthusiasm.

The funding strategy must include a budget line for SMS team compensation, contractor support, creative production, and product support.

### 5.7 In-kind must be counted

In-kind venue, tech, marketing, media, and hospitality support should be tracked as real value.

This helps show the full economic picture of the event and prevents undercounting community contribution.

## 6. Funding architecture

The formal proposal should define three budget levels.

### 6.1 Minimum viable budget

This is the version that allows TCSW 2026 to happen safely, credibly, and without harming BETA.

It should cover:

- SMS activation / production retainer;
- essential contractors;
- minimum venue costs;
- basic hospitality;
- essential marketing assets;
- registration / attendee communications support;
- basic signage and wayfinding;
- app TCSW MVP support;
- contingency.

The minimum viable budget should not include promises that depend on stretch funding.

### 6.2 Target budget

This is the version that allows TCSW 2026 to feel professionally rebuilt.

It should cover:

- stronger venue model;
- full SMS production team compensation;
- Rachel's production contractors;
- Matthieu's creative production support;
- Abhishek's product support needs;
- stronger campaign and landing page execution;
- sponsor experience delivery;
- better hospitality;
- volunteer support;
- post-event recap;
- contingency.

### 6.3 Stretch budget

This is the version that allows TCSW 2026 to become highly visible and culturally resonant.

It may include:

- larger anchor venue;
- stronger media / PR push;
- expanded lead-up events;
- higher-quality signage and environmental design;
- stronger video / photography capture;
- expanded app engagement;
- sponsor-hosted lounges or meeting zones;
- shuttle / transportation support, if needed;
- stronger post-event reporting and sponsor impact materials.

## 8. Sponsor pipeline strategy

Sponsor outreach should be divided into four pipelines.

### 8.1 Past sponsors / reunion targets

These are organizations with historical TCSW involvement.

Weldon's research package should be used to identify:

- past title sponsors;
- past Fortune 500 sponsors;
- recurring sponsors;
- sponsors who disappeared;
- sponsors who may need trust repair;
- sponsors aligned with AI, talent, founder access, or civic innovation.

Message:

> **"TCSW is being rebuilt. This is the year to come back into the room."**

### 8.2 Strategic industry sponsors

These are sponsors connected to key themes:

- AI;
- healthcare;
- climate;
- food / agriculture;
- fintech;
- education;
- corporate innovation;
- talent;
- venture / capital;
- public sector innovation.

Message:

> **"Own a visible role in the conversation your industry needs to have right now."**

### 8.3 In-kind infrastructure partners

These partners reduce event cost or increase delivery quality.

Possible categories:

- venues;
- technology;
- printing;
- media;
- hospitality;
- transportation;
- AV;
- photography;
- community promotion;
- volunteer support.

Message:

> **"Help make the rebuild possible through a visible contribution of infrastructure."**

### 8.4 Community programming partners

These partners bring audience, content, credibility, and program energy.

They may not all contribute cash, but they increase attendance, legitimacy, and sponsor value.

Message:

> **"Bring your community into the week and help fill the room."**

## 13. What not to do

Do not:

- lead with desperation;
- sell a vague startup festival;
- promise attendance before the funnel exists;
- promise room access before venues are secured;
- treat all sponsors as interchangeable;
- bury BETA's nonprofit equity;
- frame SMS as using TCSW to launch the app;
- ask Angela to close without better materials;
- let sponsor packages outrun production reality;
- accept unpaid scope that puts SMS at risk.

---

## 14. Board-safe funding language

Use:

> **The goal is not to raise money for a patched-together event. The goal is to rebuild TCSW as a credible, fundable, and trustworthy gathering point for Minnesota's startup and technology ecosystem.**

Use:

> **Sponsor dollars should protect BETA, compensate the people carrying production, and deliver real value to the community.**

Use:

> **SMS will help make the event fundable by making the public experience coherent, professionally held, and shareable.**

Avoid:

- BETA cannot do this without us;
- sponsors owe this to the community;
- we will make it huge;
- the app is the main sponsor product;
- we can figure out budget later;
- funding will come because the idea is good.

## 17. Final funding thesis

> **TCSW will not be funded because it needs to exist. It will be funded when sponsors believe this is the comeback year and that their organization should be visibly associated with bringing Minnesota's builders together again.**

The job of SMS is to help make that belief credible.

The job of BETA is to steward the trust and close the right relationships.

The job of the funding strategy is to turn latent value into committed support without overpromising, underpricing, or putting BETA back in harm's way.
